# Atlas Business Model

## Authoritative commercial structure

Atlas is a business-to-business software platform sold to law firms as a recurring subscription. The subscribing firm—not an individual case, client, AI conversation, or outside provider—is the customer tenant and commercial account.

One owner or administrator creates the firm account and subscription. Attorneys, paralegals, billing professionals, administrators, and other authorized personnel use seats within that same firm. They share the firm's authorized platform functions and digital twin according to role, case restrictions, and ethical walls. Every other firm remains technically and contractually isolated.

The core product is one maintained Atlas platform. A firm's configuration, templates, learned preferences, integrations, and private memory customize that firm's experience but do not fork or alter the shared core for other customers.

## Core subscription value

The subscription provides the connected legal operating system and private digital twin, including the agreed release scope for:

- cases, contacts, documents, forms, communications, email, calendar, tasks, events, deadlines, accounting, research, audit, and firm administration;
- native event processing, situational awareness, Workspace, `What do you need?`, Attorney Inbox, and human-reviewed AI work;
- firm data storage, retrieval, security boundaries, exports, and authorized integrations;
- continued provider interchangeability rather than dependence on one AI vendor.

The product is expected to support an initial firm-level subscription plus included or separately priced seats. The current pilot code uses a trialing `pilot` plan with a default ten-seat limit; that is an implementation default, not approved public pricing.

## Permitted revenue components

Final prices require an explicit commercial decision. The platform may support these transparent revenue components without changing the constitutional product boundaries:

1. Base firm subscription.
2. Additional professional or staff seats.
3. Clearly disclosed usage tiers for AI computation, document processing, storage, communications, or unusually high-volume integrations.
4. Paid onboarding, migration, data normalization, and implementation assistance.
5. Premium connectors, licensed-research access, telephony, texting, signature, notary, or other vendor-backed capabilities when their costs and terms justify an add-on.
6. Payment-processing, bank-connection, crypto-verification, or legal-financing integration revenue only when lawful, contractually disclosed, ethically reviewed, and delivered through the non-custodial provider boundaries.
7. Enterprise services such as advanced security, retention, data residency, support, integration, or negotiated deployment requirements.

Third-party pass-through charges must be distinguishable from Atlas subscription revenue. Atlas must not imply that a licensed research database, carrier, lender, bank, processor, blockchain network, signature provider, or notary service is included when a separate contract or charge is required.

## Prohibited monetization

Atlas does not monetize by:

- selling firm, client, case, document, communication, or attorney-work data;
- targeting advertising from confidential or privileged activity;
- allowing one firm to retrieve another firm's private memory;
- training a shared model on raw identifiable customer content without an independently approved lawful program;
- steering legal judgment to increase transaction revenue;
- holding client funds, underwriting loans, originating credit, storing raw payment credentials, or taking custody of crypto unless Atlas intentionally enters the regulated business after separate approval.

## Migration and coexistence strategy

Migration is a customer-acquisition and transition capability. A firm may connect supported existing systems through OAuth or provider-issued APIs, or preview supported exports before importing them. Atlas preserves source identity and coexistence so the firm can adopt the digital twin before abandoning its current CMS. Atlas never asks the firm to provide another provider's username and password directly.

## Shared product improvement

The commercial advantage of Atlas should compound through better generic capabilities and each firm's growing private twin. Cross-customer improvement follows the privacy firewall in the Product Constitution. Confidential data remains private; only permitted, minimized, sufficiently aggregated product signals may improve the shared platform.

## Decisions still requiring approval

The following are intentionally not invented in this document:

- public plan names and monthly or annual prices;
- included seat counts after the pilot;
- seat prices by professional role;
- included AI, storage, document, text, call, or research allowances;
- overage prices;
- onboarding and migration fees;
- processor, lender, or marketplace economics;
- trial duration, discounts, contracts, cancellation, and service-level commitments.

Until those decisions are explicitly approved and recorded, UI copy and sales materials must not present implementation defaults as commercial commitments.
